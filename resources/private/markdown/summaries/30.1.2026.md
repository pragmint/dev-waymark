This website summarizes findings from Pragmint’s review of Atlas, informed by interviews with engineers, QA, product, and engineering leadership, along with workflow and system analysis, conducted in January 2026.

The observations reflect one external perspective and should be considered alongside Atlas’s internal context, constraints, and priorities.

The purpose of this website is to:

* Identify improvement areas most likely to increase delivery speed and reliability.
* Provide a repeatable framework for prioritizing and executing improvements over time.

## The S.T.E.P. Improvement Framework

Pragmint structures improvement using a cyclical model called S.T.E.P.:

* **Survey** - Assess current capabilities objectively
* **Target** - Identify the most impactful constraints
* **Experiment** - Trial concrete practices to address those constraints
* **Polish** - Refine, adapt, and institutionalize what works

S.T.E.P. replaces ad hoc improvement efforts with shared alignment on where to focus and what to try next. Over time, it turns improvement into a repeatable capability rather than a coordination or political challenge.

Our initial assessment completes the Survey and Target phases of the initial S.T.E.P. cycle and provides inputs for beginning the Experiment phase with your team.

## Survey: Current State Summary

Leadership has already flagged the big themes: the previous implementation of micro-services/micro-frontends increased complexity and infrastructure costs, CI/CD pipelines are fragile, and observability is weak. We uncovered eight additional findings, which should make it easier to target improvements that will reduce lead time and improve platform stability. These are systemic patterns shaped by structure and incentives, not individual performance issues.

### 1. The deployment approval process has a measurable lead-time tax.

One team described releases (including hotfixes) requiring ~4+ approvers (release champion, product, eng leader, security, QA), in some cases adding ~2–3 days to lead time. This is especially costly given the current hotfix culture; “approval friction” applies repeatedly, not occasionally.

### 2. Traceability breaks at the boundary between the “new app” and microservice-land.

Teams have trace IDs available in Azure App Insights, but cross-system debugging often falls back to timestamps because trace/log correlation isn’t consistent across boundaries. Because engineers can’t reliably follow a request end to end, they lack confidence diagnosing failures that cross system boundaries. This uncertainty shows up later as hesitancy to release and reliance on manual approval gates as a risk-management substitute.

### 3. Environment configuration is a recurring production failure mode.

Environment variable management is described as: developers submit a form, then SRE/DevOps manually configures env vars per environment. Multiple people independently pointed to this as a cause of “works in lower env, fails in prod” incidents.

### 4. The team doesn’t have access to address CI/CD flakiness.

Backend deployments fail randomly and frequently. One engineer pointed to a common failure point (apply-kubectl step) but doesn’t have the access to fix the problem. The team winds up rerunning the pipelines to “fix” it.

### 5. Feature flag sprawl is extreme and actively destabilizes environment parity.

Reported counts in production: 22 flags in PostHog and 432 in Azure.

Multiple teams reported environments drifting because flag configuration differs and expectations aren’t trackable. This is a hidden coupling layer across environments and teams.

### 6. Production access is inconsistent and culturally “mid-transition.”

Several engineers still describe production access as blocked (SRE pairing required), while another reported a new hire received prod access on day one. That inconsistency is important: It will produce uneven incident response capability and confusion about operating norms unless intentionally clarified.

### 7. Incident response is “swarm-only,” with no triage protecting focus.

Multiple sources described a shared incident channel that everyone monitors, causing regular interruptions. One explicitly called out no P0/P1 triage, leading to universal disruption. This is a concrete operational pattern that explains why WIP stays high and improvement work becomes “shadow work.”

### 8. Test automation is acting as a merge gate, not a risk-reduction mechanism.

Multiple teams described writing automated tests primarily to satisfy pipeline requirements, not to validate real behavior. AI is frequently used to generate tests that are “good enough to pass the compliance step,” rather than being actually useful. As a result, teams report low confidence in test suites, yet still rely on them as formal gates. This dynamic pushes risk downstream into manual QA, approval-heavy releases, hotfixes, and production incidents, reinforcing the need for coordination and caution rather than enabling faster, safer change.

## Target: Priority Capability Areas

Using Pragmint’s assessment framework, the platform was evaluated against a set of research-backed engineering capabilities. You can see the detailed breakdown of scores and justifications in the expanded capabilities view.

The following capabilities represent the highest-leverage opportunities for improvement in the next cycle:

Capabilities that will **increase focus and alignment to reduce lead time**:

* Visibility of Work in the Value Stream
* Work in Process Limits
* Streamlining Change Approval
* Visual Management

Capabilities that will **increase troubleshooting and deployment effectiveness**:

* Monitoring and Observability
* Test Automation
* Test Data Management

Capabilities that will **streamline and simplify the release process**:

* Deployment Automation
* Continuous Integration
* Continuous Delivery

To keep the cycle tight, treat these capability groups as separate constraints, not parallel workstreams. Pick one category, drive it to a visible reduction in friction (time-to-approve, time-to-debug, time-to-release), then move to the next. Addressing them sequentially allows each improvement to reinforce the next, rather than forcing the work required to compete for attention.

## Experiment: Practice-Driven Improvement

We curated a set of experiments for your teams to consider running. They each correspond with an engineering practice that supports one or more of the capabilities targeted in the previous section. You can view them on the various teams page.

## Polish and Iterate

Not all experiments will succeed, and that is expected. After teams have had some time to run these experiments, reflect on what worked, what didn’t, and what was learned, then return to the Survey phase to re-score and uncover the next set of constraints.

## How Pragmint Can Support

Several of the above experiments require cross-functional coordination (SRE/DevOps, product approvers, security) and “muscle-building” in the team (instrumentation standards, config-as-code habits, triage mechanics). Pragmint can help implement these changes through targeted coaching/enablement or embedded Co-Dev Coaching.
